Enterprise · Publishing & Education

The Invisible Architecture-Review Queue

Problem

Leadership believed engineering had a productivity problem — stories carried over sprint after sprint, velocity was unpredictable, release dates slipped. The proposed remedy was more estimation workshops and tighter commitments.

Intervention

Made the architecture-review queue visible with explicit metrics and service-level expectations. Delegated low-risk decisions back to teams and reserved architecture involvement for genuinely high-impact items.

Diagnosis

Six months of delivery data showed dev and test cycle times were fine. The real signal was a large, highly variable delay between 'ready for development' and 'actually started' — caused by a mandatory architecture-approval step that nobody counted, tracked, or even considered a bottleneck.

Outcome

~40% reduction in end-to-end lead time without increasing engineering capacity. Pre-development wait dropped from ~10–15 business days to ~2–5. Planning became credible. Escalation traffic dropped sharply.

Lesson

"Teams rarely behave irrationally. Systems often do. Predictability didn't come from tighter control — it came from making the system observable."

Enterprise · Banking

Kanban at Scale With Almost No Leverage

Problem

A large regulated bank needed to improve operational and infrastructure delivery across dozens of teams. Many teams tracked critical work in notebooks, spreadsheets, and individual memory — the system literally could not see its own work.

Intervention

Ran coaching itself as a flow system — time-boxed engagements per team, explicit policies, flow metrics, internal champions, then a lighter-touch handover. Built a distributed improvement network rather than a few optimized islands.

Outcome

Work moved out of notebooks and into a visible, governable system. Managers shifted from anecdotal status reporting to flow-based operational decisions using measurable arrival rates and completion trends.

Lesson

"The goal isn't less control. It's better control. Success was measured by how many teams no longer needed us."

Global · IT-Services Consultancy

Delivery Across an Enterprise You Don't Own

Problem

Client executives at multinational enterprises had no single credible view of delivery risk across distributed teams, vendors, and time zones. Each team held a different picture of reality. Dependencies were discovered late, as surprises.

Intervention

Owned the spaces between teams — surfacing cross-team dependencies early enough to manage rather than escalate. Built a single shared view of delivery risk across product, delivery, and client stakeholders.

Outcome

Client executives had one credible picture of what would ship and when. Teams shifted from reaction to execution. Dependency-related surprises decreased significantly.

Lesson

"The larger the organization, the less important authority becomes and the more important alignment becomes. The most effective move is usually interpretation before solution."
